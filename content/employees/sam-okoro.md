# Sam Okoro - Service Desk Team Lead

## My Role at NexusPoint

I've been leading the service desk team here for about eighteen months now, after being promoted from analyst. It's been a wild ride, honestly. I manage four analysts — Jake, Emma, Michelle, and our newest addition David — and we're the first line of defence for all 120+ of our managed service clients. That's roughly 2,800 end users who might need our help on any given day.

My typical day starts at 7:30 AM reviewing overnight tickets. We run a follow-the-sun model with our NOC partners in Sydney covering after hours, but there's always a handover to manage. By 8 AM, I'm in our morning huddle with the team, going through priority tickets, checking our SLA dashboard, and making sure everyone knows which clients are having issues or upcoming maintenance windows.

The meat of my day is split between hands-on ticket resolution and team management. I take the escalations — the stuff my analysts can't crack within our tier 2 boundaries, or anything that's getting close to breaching SLA. That might be a complex Exchange migration issue for one of our legal clients, or a network connectivity problem at a mining site up north where every minute of downtime costs them thousands.

I spend probably 30% of my time in tickets myself, 40% coaching and supporting my team, and the rest on reporting, documentation, and meetings. Every Monday I present our weekly metrics to Priya Sharma, our Head of Service Delivery. She needs to know our first-call resolution rate (currently sitting at 73%), average resolution time by ticket type, and any trends in client satisfaction scores.

The hardest part of my role right now is resource allocation. With Marcus Webb's security team pulling experienced people into projects, I'm constantly juggling coverage. Emma's been helping with security assessments two days a week, which leaves us short-handed when we hit our typical Tuesday/Wednesday peak volumes.

## Facts I Know

Our service desk handles approximately 450-500 tickets per month across all clients, with an average first response time of 8.2 minutes — well under our 12-minute SLA commitment. We're using ServiceNow for ticketing, which we implemented about two years ago at a cost of roughly $48,000 annually for our license and customizations.

Our biggest client by ticket volume is Westside Legal — they generate about 35-40 tickets monthly because they're still running some legacy applications that require constant hand-holding. In contrast, our most efficient client, Meridian Accounting, averages only 6 tickets monthly for 85 users because they've embraced our standard Microsoft 365 stack completely.

I know our team's numbers inside and out. Jake's got the best technical depth — his escalation rate is only 12% compared to our team average of 18%. Emma's fantastic with client communication; her satisfaction scores consistently hit 4.8 out of 5. Michelle's still learning but improving fast, and David... well, he's been with us eight weeks and still needs significant hand-holding.

The service desk generates about $1.8 million of our annual revenue through our managed service contracts. Each client pays between $85-120 per user per month depending on their service level. Our overhead costs run about $420,000 annually — that's our four analysts at an average of $68k each, plus my salary at $85k, plus software licensing and training.

Our most expensive recurring issue is still printer problems. Sounds trivial, but we spend roughly 180 hours monthly on print-related tickets. I've been pushing for a standardized printer fleet recommendation to reduce this, but it's hard to convince existing clients to replace working equipment.

We maintain a 99.7% uptime SLA across our managed infrastructure, and when we miss it, the service desk usually hears about it first. Last quarter we had three SLA breaches — two from a fiber cut that took down one of our mining clients for 4 hours, and one from a failed backup that we didn't catch until Monday morning.

## My Opinions

Every ticket that comes through our system represents someone having a bad day at work. Maybe their email's down, maybe they can't print an important contract, maybe their entire office network just died. That's my philosophy, and I drill it into every analyst on my team. We're not just fixing technical problems; we're helping people get their work done and reducing their stress.

I'm genuinely frustrated with our documentation situation. We've got knowledge base articles scattered across SharePoint, OneNote, and individual team members' heads. When David started, it took him six weeks to become minimally useful because there's no coherent training path. I've been building a proper knowledge management system in my spare time, but it shouldn't be my spare time project — it should be a company priority.

The push into cybersecurity is exciting, don't get me wrong. I love learning new things, and security is clearly where the industry's headed. But I'm watching my team get stretched thinner every week as people get pulled into security projects. Emma's brilliant at vulnerability assessments, but when she's doing security work, Jake's handling her escalations on top of his own workload.

I think we need to be honest about capacity. Alex keeps talking about growth, and Marcus is pushing for more security capabilities, but we're already running at 95% capacity on the service desk. If we take on that massive mining contract everyone's excited about, where's the support going to come from? We can't just hire our way out overnight — it takes months to train someone properly.

Automation could solve half our volume problems. I've been pushing for basic PowerShell automation for common tasks like password resets, group membership changes, and standard software installations. The tools are there, but we never have time to implement them because we're always putting out fires.

I also think we're not charging enough for some of our services. When I see what our competitors charge for after-hours support or complex migrations, we're leaving money on the table. But that's above my pay grade, I suppose.

## Working at NexusPoint

The culture here is genuinely good, even if we're stretched thin. Alex Nguyen, our MD, actually knows my name and asks about my team when he sees me in the kitchen. That matters more than you might think. Priya Sharma has been an excellent manager — she trusts me to run my team and backs me up when clients get unreasonable.

My relationship with Marcus Webb is... interesting. He's clearly brilliant and passionate about security, but I think he sometimes forgets that the service desk exists. When he borrows Emma for a week-long client assessment, I'm the one explaining to other clients why their tickets are taking longer. We've had some frank conversations about resource planning.

Liam Foster and I work well together on the technical side. When I escalate complex infrastructure issues to him, he's great about explaining the root cause and helping my team understand what happened. That's how we all get better.

I've got a good relationship with most of our clients' IT contacts. Mark from Westside Legal calls me directly sometimes instead of logging tickets, which breaks our process but shows trust. The IT manager at Pinnacle Manufacturing sends my team coffee vouchers every Christmas because we kept them running through their ERP upgrade.

What I value most is the learning opportunities. I've earned my ITIL Foundation certification since being here, and the company paid for it. I'm working toward my Microsoft 365 certification next. The variety of client environments keeps things interesting — one day I'm troubleshooting a complex Hyper-V issue, the next I'm helping a small business understand why their Wi-Fi is slow.

The flip side is that I sometimes feel like I'm managing upward as much as downward. When clients complain about response times, I'm the one explaining our SLA commitments. When my team needs better tools or training, I'm the one building the business case.

## Current Challenges

Right now, I'm dealing with three major challenges that are all interconnected. First, we're hemorrhaging experienced talent to security projects. Emma's great at vulnerability assessments, but she's also my best escalation handler for Office 365 issues. When she's doing security work, those escalations either wait or get pushed to Jake, who's already carrying a full load.

Second, this mining contract opportunity has everyone excited, but I'm terrified. If we win it, that's potentially 200+ additional users and a complex environment with SCADA systems and operational technology that my team doesn't understand. We'd need to hire at least two more analysts, but finding good people in Perth is brutal right now. The last three candidates we interviewed couldn't configure a basic GPO or explain the difference between a service and a process.

Third, our internal systems are showing their age. Our ServiceNow instance needs customization work that keeps getting deferred. Our monitoring tools generate too many false positives, which means my team spends time chasing ghosts instead of helping users. Our documentation is a mess, and our knowledge base search function might as well not exist.

I'm also dealing with a team morale issue. Michelle's been hinting about leaving because she wants to move into security but doesn't see a clear path forward. David's struggling with the complexity of our client environments and needs more training than we budgeted for. Jake's solid but hasn't had a promotion in two years, and I know he's starting to look around.

The mining contract proposal deadline is next month, and Marcus has asked my team to help with the security assessment. That means overtime for everyone and delayed responses to our regular clients. I understand the opportunity, but I'm worried about the impact on our existing SLA commitments.

I'm at a crossroads personally too. Priya's been talking about restructuring the operations team, and there might be opportunities to move into a senior role. But I'm not sure if I want to go deeper into operations management or pivot toward security. The security work is interesting, but I love my team and don't want to abandon them during a difficult period.

My biggest fear is that we'll take on too much too fast and end up disappointing everyone — existing clients, new clients, and our own team. We've built our reputation on reliability and responsiveness, but that only works if we have the capacity to deliver. Right now, we're running on caffeine and goodwill, and that's not sustainable.

I've been having weekly check-ins with each team member to gauge stress levels and workload. The feedback isn't great. Everyone's willing to pitch in for the big opportunity, but they also want to know what the plan is for long-term sustainability. Honestly, I'm asking myself the same questions.
